Surrealism

A brief introduction:

Surrealism was an avant-garde cultural movement that emerged in the early 20th century, primarily in the 1920s, and is best known for its exploration of the unconscious mind, dreams, and irrational imagery. Influenced by the psychoanalytic theories of Sigmund Freud, surrealist artists and writers sought to challenge conventional reality by expressing the illogical, fantastical, and absurd. Surrealism aimed to break free from traditional artistic boundaries and to explore the deeper layers of human experience, often using unexpected juxtapositions, bizarre scenarios, and distorted representations of reality. Key figures of the movement included artists like Salvador Dalí, René Magritte, and Max Ernst, and writers such as André Breton, who authored the Surrealist Manifesto. The movement had a lasting influence on visual arts, literature, film, and even politics, offering a radical departure from rational thought and celebrating the power of imagination and spontaneity.
